# Abstract

**Lateral-Sway-Driven Clearance in Straight-Legged Bipedal Walking: A Coupled Pendulum Model**

Ground clearance during bipedal walking is conventionally achieved through knee flexion, hip drop, or active leg shortening — each introducing mechanical complexity that raises questions about the minimal requirements for sustained locomotion. This paper investigates an alternative mechanism: that lateral body sway over the stance leg is sufficient, on its own, to lift the opposite hip and create clearance for a fully rigid swing leg, eliminating the need for a knee joint entirely.

The proposed model treats walking as the coordinated interaction of two pendulum motions. The body rocks laterally over the stance foot, elevating the swing-side hip, which then allows the straight swing leg to pass forward freely without ground contact. These two motions — lateral rock and forward swing — are not independent; the clearance available to the swing leg is directly determined by how far the body sways. Understanding and exploiting this coupling is the central focus of the work.

We derive the conditions under which this mechanism produces stable, periodic walking, and examine the trade-off between sway magnitude, stride length, and energy cost. The results suggest that lateral motion in walking is not merely a balance problem to be corrected, but a functional contributor to the gait cycle itself.
